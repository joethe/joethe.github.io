Home Lab

Calling it a "lab" may be a bit of a stretch, but that's what I like to think of it as. This is a description of my humble collection of computing equipment.

Storage Server

Born partly of a genuine need for more storage, and partly of a desire to further separate my storage from my desktop computer and whatever ailments befall it. Currently, I trust my data to a bargain-bin motherboard sporting an AMD Phenom II X2 560 processor with a whole 2 cores, running at a whopping 3.3 GHz. All of this is housed in a cheap 4U rack-mount case with some hotswap bays. It runs CentOS, and the data is kept (relatively) safe by SnapRaid.

PFSense Firewall

A dubious violation of University network usage policy, a little HP Compaq desktop salvaged from the dumpster serves as a firewall and router for my tiny little network, allowing me to isolate some of my stuff from the outside world while still keeping networking configurations on my individual pieces of equipment simple. Although, most importantly, in combination with a switch it allows me to connect more than one thing via wired Ethernet without making anyone too angry.*

Old HP Desktops

My intention was to set these up to compile a Linux kernel in parallel, hoping to speed up the process somewhat. This hasn't happened yet for a variety of reasons - primarily that I have nowhere to put them. I guess I haven't really found time or motivation to do any work that would require kernel compiling either.

Virtual Environment

Not being a fan of having to constantly re-install operating systems when I inevitably screw them up while playing around, at some point I decided to dedicate some hardware to hosting a virtual-environment for running Linux containers and VMs.

  • CPU: AMD FX6300 6 core 3.5GHz
  • Motherboard: (will update when I find the box, but it's nothing special)
  • Memory: 4GB of generic DDR3 (single stick) Now it has 8GB of generic memory! (2 4GB sticks)
  • Storage: 500GB generic hard drive
  • Case:(will update when I remember...)
  • Operating System: Proxmox VE

Why Proxmox? Why Not VMware?

I choose Proxmox primarily because of this Tek Syndicate video. I like open source things, and make an attempt to use them whenever possible as I seem to learn more using them than when using their (usually) more popular counterparts. However now that I've used Proxmox a bit I feel that I can better appreciate VMware, and will try messing around with it in the future.
